FAQ

What is the difference between PMS and PMDD?

PMS (premenstrual syndrome) causes physical and mood symptoms in the luteal phase that resolve when bleeding starts. PMDD is a severe form where symptoms significantly impair work, relationships, or daily function — ACOG estimates it affects 3–8% of menstruating women and may require medical treatment.

How do I know if my period is too heavy?

NHS and ACOG define heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) as soaking through a pad or tampon every hour for several hours, needing double protection, passing clots larger than a quarter, or bleeding longer than 7 days. Chronic heavy bleeding can cause iron-deficiency anemia.

Could severe cramps mean endometriosis?

Primary dysmenorrhea (normal cramps) often improves with NSAIDs and heat. Endometriosis is suspected when pain is severe, worsens over time, occurs outside menstruation, or is paired with pain during sex or bowel movements. Diagnosis requires evaluation by a gynecologist — often via laparoscopy.

Why do symptoms change across my cycle?

Hormone levels shift throughout the cycle. Estrogen rises in the follicular phase; progesterone dominates the luteal phase. Many people feel bloating, mood changes, and breast tenderness in the week before bleeding when progesterone drops — the classic PMS window tracked by apps like Clue and Flo.

When should I track symptoms before seeing a doctor?

Keep a simple log for 2–3 cycles: cycle length, bleeding days, pain score, and mood. Bring this to your appointment — it helps distinguish PMS, PCOS, thyroid issues, or fibroids much faster than a single visit alone.
